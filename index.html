Software is Different

Product Lifetimes

Independent Products vs. Continuous Improvement

  • Unlike hardware, software is expected to grow and evolve over time.
  • Customers expect a hardware product to be bug-free. If it's not working properly, it should be replaced.
  • Customers are considerably more forgiving for software.

Product Lifetimes

Independent Products vs. Continuous Improvement

  • Legacy Code: Continues to be used because it meets customers’ needs.
  • 60% of software maintenance costs are for adding new functionality to legacy software. It's successful!
  • Beautiful Code: Long-lasting code that is easy to evolve.
  • Worst case is unexpectedly short-lived code that is soon discarded because it doesn’t meet customers’ needs.

Development Processes

Plan-and-Document vs. Agile

  • Software processes initially followed hardware processes. Measure twice, cut once!
  • Waterfall Model: A sequential process through phases of analysis, design, implementation, testing, deployment and maintenance.
  • Works well with well-defined tasks like NASA space flights, but it runs into trouble when customers change their minds.

Development Processes

Plan-and-Document vs. Agile

  • Spiral Model: Iterates through all the development phases to produce prototypes with iterations of 6 to 24 months long.
  • All Plan-and-Document models rely on careful planning and thorough documentation, and measure progress against a plan.

Agile Model

Agile Model

  • In February 2001, 17 software developers met to find a common ground for lightweight development methods and published the Agile Manifesto
www.agilemanifesto.org

Agile Model

  • Continuous refinement of a working but incomplete prototype until the customer is happy.
  • Documentation is primarily through user stories, and progress is measured by recording velocity.
  • Agile processes include Extreme Programming, Scrum, Kanban, Scrumban, Lean, etc.

Scrum
Scrum (n): A framework within which people can address complex adaptive problems, while productively and creatively delivering products of the highest possible value.
The Scrum Guide

Scrum Theory

  • Scrum is founded on empirical process control theory.
  • Empiricism asserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is known.
  • Scrum employs an iterative, incremental approach to optimize predictability and control risk.
  • Three pillars: transparency, inspection, and adaptation.

Scrum Theory

  • Transparency: Significant aspects of the process must be visible to those responsible for the outcome.
  • Inspection: Users must frequently inspect artifacts and progress toward a Sprint Goal to detect undesirable variances.
  • Adaptation: If some aspects of a process deviate outside acceptable limits, an adjustment must be made as soon as possible to minimize further deviation.

Scrum Values

  • Commitment: People commit to achieving team goals.
  • Courage: Members have courage to do the right thing.
  • Focus: Everyone focuses on the work of the Sprint.
  • Openness: Memebrs and stakeholders agree to be open about all the work and challenges.
  • Respect: Members respect each other to be capable, independent people.

Scrum Artifacts

  • Scrum’s artifacts provide transparency and opportunities for inspection and adaptation.
  • Artifacts defined by Scrum are specifically designed to maximize transparency of key information so that everybody has the same understanding of the artifact.

1. Product Backlog

  • An ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product including features, functions, requirements, enhancements, and fixes.
  • Dynamic and is never complete.
  • Product Backlog refinement: an ongoing process of adding detail, estimates, and order to items.

2. Sprint Backlog

  • The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering the product Increment and realizing the Sprint Goal.
  • Emerges during the Sprint as the team works through the plan and learns more about the work needed to achieve the Sprint Goal.

3. Increment

  • The sum of all the Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint and the value of the increments of all previous Sprints.
  • Must be in useable condition regardless of whether it will be actually released.

Definition of "Done"

  • Team members must have a shared understanding of what it means for work to be complete, to ensure transparency.

The Scrum Team

  • Self-organizing: Team members choose how best to accomplish their work, rather than being directed by others outside the team.
  • Cross-functional: Team members have all competencies needed to accomplish the work without depending on others not part of the team.

1. The Product Owner

  • Responsible for maximizing the value of the product and the work of the Development Team.
  • Responsible for managing the Product Backlog:
    • Clearly expresses Product Backlog items.
    • Orders Product Backlog items to best achieve goals.
    • Ensures that the Product Backlog is visible, transparent, and clear to all.

2. The Development Team

  • 3 to 9 professionals who deliver a potentially releasable Increment at the end of each Sprint.
  • Self-organizing and cross-functional.
  • No titles for members other than "Developer", regardless of the work being performed by the person.
  • Members may have specialized skills, but accountability belongs to the team as a whole.

3. The Scrum Master

  • Responsible for ensuring Scrum is understood and enacted by ensuring that the Scrum Team adheres to Scrum theory, practices, and rules.
  • Finds techniques for effective Product Backlog management.
  • Coaches the Development Team in self-organization and cross-functionality.
  • Works with other Scrum Masters to increase the effectiveness of Scrum in the organization.

Scrum Events

  • Time-boxed events to create regularity and to minimize the need for additional meetings.
  • Failure to include any of the events results in reduced transparency and is a lost opportunity to inspect and adapt.

1. The Sprint

  • The heart of Scrum and container for all other events.
  • A time-box of one month or less during which a "Done", useable, and potentially releasable product Increment is created.
  • Sprints have consistent durations throughout a development effort.
  • During the Sprint, scope may be clarified, but no changes are made that would endanger the Sprint Goal

2. Sprint Planning

  • The work to be performed in the Sprint is planned collaboratively by the entire Scrum Team:
    • What can be done in this Sprint?
      Selected Product Backlog items + Sprint Goal
    • How will the Sprint Goal be achieved?
  • 8 hours for a one-month Sprint.

3. Daily Scrum

  • 15-minute for the Development Team to synchronize activities and create a plan for the next 24 hours:
    • What did I do yesterday that helped the Development Team meet the Sprint Goal?
    • What will I do today to help the Development Team meet the Sprint Goal?
    • Do I see any impediment that prevents me or the Development Team from meeting the Sprint Goal?

4. Sprint Review

  • The Scrum Team and key stakeholders meet at the end of the Sprint to inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog if needed.
  • The Product Owner explains what have been "Done".
  • The Development Team demonstrates the work that it has "Done" and discusses the problems it ran into.
  • The entire group collaborates on what to do next.
  • 4 hours for a one-month Sprint.

5. Sprint Retrospective

  • The Scrum Team meets after the Sprint Review to:
    • Inspect how the last Sprint went with regards to people, relationships, process, and tools.
    • Create a plan for implementing improvements.
  • 3 hours for a one-month Sprint.
